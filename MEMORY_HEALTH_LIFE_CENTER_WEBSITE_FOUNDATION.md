## 1. Executive Summary

The redesigned website exists to convert public interest, donor capacity, and political momentum into measurable support for the **Memory Health Life Center (MHLC)** — a proposed 54-bed, Hogeweyk-inspired memory care, education, and workforce-development campus in Quitman, Texas, stewarded by the **Wood County Health Care Foundation (WCHCF)**.

The site must do nine things well:

1. **Explain the vision clearly** in plain English so a first-time visitor (donor, official, family member, journalist) understands what is being built and why within 30 seconds of landing.
2. **Build credibility** with major donors, family foundations, state and federal officials, health care partners, and East Texas families by foregrounding governance, partnerships, and stewardship.
3. **Support fundraising** with a frictionless, mobile-first donation path, clear giving levels, and a major-gifts/naming-opportunity track for transformational donors.
4. **Show project progress** as design, permitting, capital-campaign milestones, and eventual construction unfold — turning the site into a living progress report rather than a static brochure.
5. **Communicate the human need** for better memory care in rural East Texas with dignity, hope, and specificity — never with pity or fear.
6. **Position WCHCF as a trustworthy steward** — a 501(c)(3) with a decades-long history of serving Wood County health care, now stewarding a regionally significant capital project.
7. **Make it easy** to donate, learn more, contact the foundation, request a tour or presentation, and subscribe for updates.
8. **Support advocacy** with shareable, official-friendly fact sheets and talking points to help win the state matching funds tied to the federal earmark.
9. **Serve as a workforce and education portal** for partner medical, nursing, social work, and allied-health programs — starting with UT Tyler School of Medicine.

The project sits at the intersection of rural health care, dementia care innovation, workforce development, and East Texas community-building. The site needs to read that way.

## 3. Project Understanding

### 3.1 Core Project Description

The **Memory Health Life Center** is a planned 54-bed memory care, education, and brain-health campus on a 29-acre site adjacent to UT Health Quitman Hospital in Quitman, Texas. It is being developed by the Wood County Health Care Foundation as a regional resource for East Texas. The care model is inspired by the Hogeweyk dementia village in the Netherlands and adapted to serve East Texas families — including low- and moderate-income families — in a setting that prioritizes dignity, normal daily life, and purposeful activity over institutional containment. The campus is designed to combine residential memory care with a working farm and gardens, animal interaction, walking paths, classrooms, and clinical training space for medical, nursing, and allied-health students.

### 3.2 The Human Problem

- **Alzheimer's disease and related dementias** are growing in prevalence as the East Texas population ages.
- **Rural access to specialized memory care** is severely limited; families often have to choose between under-resourced local options and long drives to urban centers.
- **Caregiver burden** in rural communities is intense and largely invisible — spouses and adult children carrying years of around-the-clock care with little professional support.
- **Existing care models** in many regional facilities default to confinement, sedation, and one-size-fits-all routines that strip away dignity and accelerate decline.
- **Workforce shortages** in dementia-competent nursing, social work, and physician care are acute in rural East Texas.
- **Families need** safety, dignity, evidence-based care, respite, education, and a place where a loved one can still have a life — not just a bed.

### 3.3 The Proposed Solution

The Memory Health Life Center is positioned as:

- A **residential memory care community** designed around the Hogeweyk/Scandinavian model of normalized daily life. **[CONFIRMED — NEWS]**
- A campus where residents can **garden, tend animals, walk, work, and gather** as part of daily life rather than being confined to a clinical floor. **[CONFIRMED — NEWS]**
- A center of **evidence-based, non-pharmacological care** for dementia — prioritizing environment, activity, social connection, and purpose. **[INFERRED from publicly stated model]**
- A **brain-health hub** addressing prevention, early diagnosis, treatment, and quality of life. **[CONFIRMED — NEWS, foundation language]**
- A **caregiver support resource** for families across the region. **[INFERRED, consistent with mission]**
- A **workforce development engine** in partnership with UT Tyler School of Medicine and (intended) other education partners. **[CONFIRMED — NEWS for UT Tyler; other partners PLACEHOLDER]**
- A **regional resource for East Texas**, not just Wood County. **[CONFIRMED — NEWS, foundation language]**
- A demonstrably more **humane and innovative model** of care — the first of its kind in the region and one of a small number nationally. **[INFERRED]**

### 3.4 Geographic and Community Context

- **Quitman, Texas** — county seat of Wood County; small, close-knit East Texas community where Mayor Randy Dunn has championed the project. **[CONFIRMED — NEWS]**
- **Wood County** — the foundation's home community and the population the foundation has served for decades.
- **East Texas** — the regional service area; the center is positioned as a resource for families across the region, with Tyler (and UT Tyler) roughly an hour away. **[CONFIRMED — NEWS]**
- **Rural communities** — the project explicitly addresses the gap in specialized memory care outside major metro areas.
- **Regional health care access** — the campus sits adjacent to the existing UT Health Quitman Hospital, integrating with an active hospital and medical school ecosystem. **[CONFIRMED — NEWS]**

## 8. Fundraising Strategy for the Website

### 8.1 Donation Path

The ideal donor journey:

1. **Land** on Home (or Project page from a press link or social share).
2. **Understand** the vision in under 30 seconds.
3. **Trust** the steward (visible board, 501(c)(3), EIN, press, partners).
4. **Believe** the project is real and progressing (timeline, federal grant, partners).
5. **Decide** to give (primary CTA always visible).
6. **Give** on a frictionless, mobile-optimized form (one-time or monthly, low-fee processor).
7. **Receive** an immediate, warm, branded thank-you with tax-deductibility info.
8. **Get followed up** with: progress email within 7 days, named contact for major gift conversations, and a regular update cadence.

## 11. Visual Design Direction

### 11.1 Overall Style
Professional, hopeful, human, warm, modern, health-care-appropriate, donor-appropriate, community-grounded. The site should feel like a place — not a brochure.

### 11.2 Color Palette Recommendations

Recommend a palette anchored in calming health care colors (deep teal, soft sage, soft sky) with warm East Texas accents (terracotta, gold, warm clay) and excellent contrast. Avoid pure clinical white-and-blue and avoid grayscale institutional treatments. Suggested working palette (for design exploration only — confirm with foundation brand):

| Role | Suggested direction |
| --- | --- |
| Primary | Deep teal or forest (trust, health, regional landscape) |
| Secondary | Warm clay / terracotta (East Texas warmth) |
| Accent | Soft gold (highlight, CTA hover) |
| Neutral background | Warm off-white / sand |
| Text | Near-black charcoal (not pure black) on warm background |
| Success / progress | Sage |
| Alert / urgency | Warm coral (sparingly) |

All combinations must meet **WCAG 2.2 AA** contrast at minimum and **AAA for body text on warm backgrounds** where feasible.

### 11.3 Typography

- **Headings:** A humanist serif (e.g., Source Serif, Lora, Tiempos) for warmth and credibility.
- **Body:** A highly legible humanist sans (e.g., Inter, Source Sans, Public Sans) at a generous size (**18px minimum body**, **20–22px preferred for older audiences**).
- **Strong heading hierarchy** (H1 → H2 → H3, no skipping).
- **Generous line height** (1.55–1.7 for body).

### 11.4 Photography and Imagery

Categories to commission and curate:

- East Texas landscape (the place — pines, light, sky).
- Real families and caregivers (with consent and releases).
- Older adults in genuine, dignified moments (not staged "sad senior" stock).
- Community gatherings and events.
- Health care professionals and students at work.
- Renderings, site plans, and architectural diagrams.
- Construction progress (once active).
- Donor and community event photography.

Avoid: stock imagery of unspecified hands holding unspecified hands; clinical hospital photography; sterile interiors.

### 11.5 Icon and Illustration Style

Simple, warm, professional line icons with rounded corners. Two-color usage (primary + accent) on warm backgrounds. Consistent stroke weight. Not cartoonish. Used to explain services, model components, and impact metrics.

### 11.6 Page Layout

- Strong hero with a single primary message.
- Persistent header CTA (Donate) and persistent footer subscription.
- Generous whitespace; mobile-first; one idea per section.
- Visual progress indicators (campaign thermometer, timeline) used sparingly and clearly.
- Modular card grids for services, partners, news.
- Timeline section as a horizontal scroll on desktop, vertical stack on mobile.
- Sticky in-page anchor nav on long pages (Project, Give).

## 13. Accessibility Requirements

The site must serve older adults, donors with low vision, and visitors with assistive technology. Minimum bar: **WCAG 2.2 AA**.

- High contrast text (AA minimum; AAA on body where feasible).
- **18px body minimum**, 20–22px preferred. Respect user font-size preferences.
- Buttons ≥ 48×48px tap target; clear button labels (no icon-only buttons without `aria-label`).
- Full keyboard navigation; visible focus rings (do not suppress).
- Alt text on all informative images; empty alt on decorative.
- Properly labeled forms; visible labels (do not rely on placeholder text alone).
- Avoid text embedded in images.
- Descriptive link text.
- Donation flow accessible end-to-end: keyboard-only completion verified.
- Mobile usability: thumb-reachable primary CTAs; no horizontal scroll.
- Older-adult-friendly readability: short paragraphs, generous line height, sentence-case.
- Captions on all video; transcripts available.
- Skip-to-content link.
- Test with at least one screen reader (NVDA or VoiceOver) before launch.

## 19. Final Recommendations

### Top 5 things the new website must accomplish
1. **Explain the project clearly enough that a stranger gets it in 30 seconds** — vision, scale, place, model, steward.
2. **Make giving frictionless on a phone**, with one-time, monthly, and major-gift paths and a visible campaign progress thermometer.
3. **Establish credibility** with named leadership, 501(c)(3) status with EIN, partner logos (UT Health Quitman, UT Tyler School of Medicine), federal funding status, and press coverage.
4. **Show momentum** with a live timeline and a steady cadence of progress updates — at minimum monthly.
5. **Serve advocacy** — equip officials, journalists, and partner organizations with a downloadable, citable fact sheet and a clear ask, with the federal/state matching-funds story front and center.

### Top 5 risks to avoid
1. **Generic "donate to a foundation" framing** that fails to make MHLC the clear protagonist.
2. **Soft-focus clinical stock imagery** and pity-driven copy that erodes dignity and credibility.
3. **Opaque finances** — vague costs, missing goal, no progress visibility — which kills major-donor confidence.
4. **A stale site** — launching the redesign without an ongoing publishing rhythm. A beautiful site that doesn't update is worse than an ugly one that does.
5. **Inaccessible donation flow** — small tap targets, low contrast, keyboard traps, or a third-party form that breaks on mobile.

### The best homepage structure (see Section 7 for detail)
Hero → Vision → Need → Model → Region & Partners → Campaign → Progress → Families → Community → Latest News → Final CTA.

### Most important missing information to gather
1. **Current campaign goal and totals** (community raised, federal status, state match status).
2. **Final project name, mission, and one-sentence pitch** approved by the foundation board.
3. **Renderings, site plan, and a project fact sheet** (PDF, citable).
4. **Full current board roster** with bios, photos, and roles.
5. **Named major-gift officer** with email, phone, and a real face.
6. **Confirmed partner list** beyond UT Health Quitman and UT Tyler School of Medicine.
7. **Opening target date** and construction-readiness status.
8. **A current donor wall / recognition policy** (opt-in language, levels, anonymity defaults).
9. **Brand assets** — logo (vector), color palette, typography, photography library.
10. **Donation platform decision** (processor + recurring + DAF + stock + planned giving).
